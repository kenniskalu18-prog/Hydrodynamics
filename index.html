Fluid Flow Visualization

Fluids can flow in two main ways: smooth streamline flow or chaotic turbulent flow. The transition between these depends on fluid properties and flow conditions.

I. Stream Flow and Types

Streamflow refers to the characteristics that determine how water moves in a stream channel. It's influenced by the source and physical objects present in the stream.

Two Main Types of Fluid Flow:

A. Streamline/Laminar Flow

  • Fluid flows in parallel layers with no disruption between them
  • Fluid particles follow straight paths parallel to pipe walls
  • Every point in the fluid follows the same path as preceding particles
  • Velocity at a given point remains constant with time
Laminar Flow Visualization

Imagine dye injected into a slowly flowing fluid - it forms a smooth, continuous line without mixing.

B. Turbulent Flow

  • Flow paths are irregular and chaotic
  • Local currents develop throughout the fluid
  • Fluid particles move in zig-zag, irregular patterns
  • Swirls (eddies) form, causing high energy loss
Turbulent Flow Visualization

Think of whitewater rapids or smoke rising from a cigarette - chaotic, mixing motion.

III. Viscosity and Stream Flow

Viscosity is a fluid property that describes its resistance to flow. It's essentially the internal friction of a fluid.

Viscosity Formula: Œ∑ = (F √ó d) / (A √ó v)

Where:

  • F = force applied (N)
  • d = distance between plates (m)
  • A = area of plates (m¬≤)
  • v = velocity (m/s)
  • Œ∑ = viscosity (Ns/m¬≤ or Pa¬∑s)

Effect of Viscosity on Flow Type:

High Viscosity (e.g., Honey)

  • Increases tendency for laminar flow
  • Resists transition to turbulence
  • Higher internal friction between fluid layers

Low Viscosity (e.g., Water)

  • Increases tendency for turbulent flow
  • Easier transition to turbulence
  • Lower internal friction between fluid layers

V. Bernoulli's Principle and Equation

Bernoulli's Principle: For an ideal fluid in steady flow, an increase in fluid velocity occurs simultaneously with a decrease in pressure or a decrease in the fluid's potential energy.

Where:

  • P = pressure (Pa or N/m¬≤)
  • œÅ = density (kg/m¬≥)
  • v = velocity (m/s)
  • g = acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s¬≤)
  • h = height above reference level (m)

Key Applications of Bernoulli's Principle:

1. Venturi Effect

When fluid flows through a constriction, its velocity increases and pressure decreases.

2. Airplane Wings

Air travels faster over the curved upper surface, creating lower pressure and lift.

3. Atomizers and Spray Bottles

Fast-moving air creates low pressure, drawing liquid up and breaking it into spray.

VI. Ideal Fluid Assumptions

An ideal fluid is a theoretical model that simplifies fluid dynamics calculations by assuming:

1. Non-viscous (No internal friction)

No resistance between fluid layers; viscosity Œ∑ = 0

2. Incompressible (Constant density)

Density œÅ remains constant regardless of pressure changes

3. Steady Flow (Non-turbulent)

Velocity, density, and pressure at each point remain constant over time

4. Irrotational Flow

Fluid has no angular momentum about any point; no turbulence

Real vs. Ideal Fluids

Real fluids have viscosity and can be compressible, but the ideal fluid model provides useful approximations for many engineering applications.
